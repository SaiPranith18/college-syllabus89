Functional vs Non-Functional Requirements

In software engineering, requirements are specifications or descriptions of what a system or software product should do or how it should behave. These requirements are typically categorized into two main types: functional and non-functional requirements.

Functional Requirements

  • User-centric: Functional requirements are derived from the needs and expectations of the system's users. They represent the desired functions or features that users expect the software to perform.
  • Behavioral Descriptions: Functional requirements define the behavior of the system under various conditions. They specify how the system should respond to different inputs and stimuli.
  • Verifiable: Functional requirements can usually be verified through testing. Test cases can be designed to check whether the system behaves as specified in the requirements.
  • Captured in Use Cases: Functional requirements are often captured in the form of use cases, which describe specific interactions between users and the system to achieve particular goals or tasks.

Non-Functional Requirements

  • Quality Attributes: Non-functional requirements define the quality attributes or properties of the system. They specify how well the system performs certain functions rather than what functions it performs.
  • Constraints on Functional Requirements: Non-functional requirements place constraints on the system's functional requirements. They dictate how the system should fulfill its functional requirements in terms of performance, reliability, and other aspects.
  • Measured by Quality Metrics: Non-functional requirements are often measured using specific quality metrics. For example, performance requirements might specify response times or throughput rates that the system must achieve under certain conditions.
  • Captured as Quality Attributes: Non-functional requirements are typically captured as quality attributes or constraints rather than specific use cases or features.

Examples

Examples of functional requirements include user authentication, data validation, report generation, and any other specific features or actions that the software must perform.

Examples of non-functional requirements include response time, system availability, security measures, and scalability.
